Mindset: it's a hire, not an app

Five sentences that carry everything else in this guide:

You don't download it, you onboard it. Like any new coworker it needs access, instructions, context, and a review period.
Everyone has the same models. The constraint is not the AI, it is the written context you give it. The best documented company wins.
It starts powerless. It can only touch what you deliberately connect. You grant reach, door by door.
You move from doing to directing. Set intent, review output, hold the send button. Production is its job; judgment and accountability stay yours.
Everything you learn here is also the product. We work this way, then we sell this way of working. Your skill is our inventory.

03

Anatomy of Claude Code

Seven concepts, one metaphor family. This is the whole customization system.

The desk

Context window. Everything the AI is currently working with sits on one desk. A cluttered desk degrades the work, for AI exactly as for people. Most of the concepts below exist to keep the desk clean.

The handbook

CLAUDE.md. Read at the start of every session, automatically. Your rules, your context, your preferences. Whatever you find yourself re-explaining, write it in here once and never again.

Playbooks

Skills. A checklist that slots onto the current desk and shapes how this specific task is done, together with you. Example: an invoice skill that knows our numbering and template. Invoked with /name.

Side desks

Agents and subagents. A coworker with their own desk. You hand off a messy pile (read these 40 pages, research this niche), the mess stays on their desk, one clean summary card comes back to yours.

Tripwires

Hooks. House rules the building enforces, not politeness the worker must remember. A hook physically blocks an action (say, a destructive command) no matter what anyone asks. Guardrails that hold at 3 a.m.

Moving boxes

Plugins. Handbook + playbooks + rules packed into one box that installs in one step. How a whole setup moves to a new person, or to a client.

USB ports

MCP. The standard plug that connects tools to the AI. Everything in the Connections chapter goes through one. New purchasing question for any software: does it have a plug?

Web app 101: what you're actually building

You will build and ship web applications with Claude Code without being career programmers. For that you need the map, not the syntax. Here is the whole map.

Every web app has three layers

Think of a restaurant:

Frontend is the dining room: everything the user sees and touches (pages, buttons, forms). Built from HTML, CSS and JavaScript.
Backend is the kitchen: the logic that runs when something is clicked. It checks, calculates, decides what's allowed. The user never sees it.
Database is the pantry and the ledger: the app's memory. Data written here survives after the page is closed. Tables of rows, like a spreadsheet with rules.

One click on "Book appointment" travels: frontend → backend (is the slot free? is the user logged in?) → database (write the booking) → back with "Booked ✓". Under a second, every time.

Services: the parts you plug in, not build

Real apps almost never do everything themselves. For standard needs you connect a service with an API key and move on:

Sending email (confirmations, notifications): Resend or AWS SES. The app says "send this", the service handles deliverability.
Users and login (auth): registration, passwords, sessions. Never invent your own; use a proven library or service (we use Better Auth in one of our stacks).
Payments: Stripe. The app receives a "paid" signal; cards, subscriptions and regulation are Stripe's problem.
File storage: uploads and images go to object storage (S3-style), not a folder on the server.
Analytics: who visits, from where, what they click.

Open source: run it on your own server

For many paid tools there is an open source equivalent you can self-host on a VPS: your data, no monthly fee, full control, and (increasingly important) an MCP plug so your AI can operate it. Our live example: Twenty CRM runs on our own server instead of a paid CRM subscription, and Claude manages records in it through MCP. Same pattern exists for analytics (Umami, Plausible), storage (MinIO), auth (Keycloak). The trade-off is honest: you own the uptime. For email and payments, stick with services.

What this means for you two

Claude Code writes and wires all of it: frontend, backend, database, service connections, deploy. Your job is the part it cannot do: know WHAT you want (spec), decide trade-offs when asked, and verify the result actually works. The vocabulary above is so you can read its plans and call nonsense when you see it. That plus the method chapter is the whole "learning to program" you need to start shipping; depth comes from reps, not courses.

The method, on one worked example

How we build anything, shown on a website. The same loop applies to a document, a campaign, or an analysis.

steal→ get grilled→ plan on a board→ prototype→ build→ verify→ ship→ recap
  1. Steal like a pro. Never start from a blank page. Have the AI collect the best sites in the niche (web search + Playwright screenshots) and pull from our swipe file. Taste is choosing well, not inventing from zero.
  2. Get grilled. Run /grill-me. The AI interviews YOU, one question at a time, each with its recommended answer, until the spec has no holes. The AI asking questions is a feature; assumptions are how projects die.
  3. Plan visually. Run /visual-plan. The plan renders as an interactive board in your browser (Lavish): options, tradeoffs, open questions. You approve by clicking, not by reading a wall of text. Nothing gets built before the board is approved.
  4. Prototype before building. The AI produces HTML design concepts with real content. You react to screens, it iterates, you lock one. The locked design is binding: the build must match it, every visible button must work.
  5. Build. Now, and only now, the production work happens. Big messy subtasks go to side desks; your main session stays clean and directs.
  6. Verify before "done". Playwright screenshots at desktop and phone width, compared against the locked design; every control clicked. "The tool said success" is not done. Looking at the output is done.
  7. Ship. Commit, push, Dokploy deploys, live URL lands in Discord.
  8. Recap and close. /visual-recap renders what changed for review, the task is closed in NativeAI OS with a one-line note, and anything you had to explain twice gets written into CLAUDE.md so next time you explain it zero times.
The five habits behind the example: write context down · plan before building · delegate mess to side desks · verify before done · get a second opinion (Codex) on expensive decisions.

First exercises: see it work

Do these in order, in your first week. The first five take minutes each and teach one load-bearing idea; the sixth is the final assignment. Paste each prompt as written.

1 · Tame a folder read → plan → approve → execute

Look at my Downloads folder. Propose an organization plan
(what groups, what gets archived, what looks like junk).
Show me the plan first; only execute after I approve.

Lesson: the AI reads real state, proposes, waits for your approval, then acts. The loop you'll use everywhere.

2 · Research with receipts web-check culture

Research the top 5 tools for <topic you know well>.
For every factual claim give the source link and the date
you fetched it, and state your confidence. No claims from memory.

Lesson: verified claims with sources, the difference between our output and AI slop. Judge it against your own knowledge of the topic.

3 · Plan on a board /grill-me + /visual-plan

I want to plan <something real you actually want to do>.
First /grill-me until you understand it fully,
then /visual-plan so I can review it as a board.

Lesson: watch it extract context out of your head and turn it into decisions you click through.

4 · A side desk context hygiene

Here is a long document / folder: <path>.
Send a subagent to read all of it and bring me back
a one-page brief with the 5 things that matter.

Lesson: the mess stays on the side desk, your session stays sharp. This is why long work doesn't degrade.

5 · Ship something the full loop

Build me a one-page site about <anything>.
Prototype first, let me react, then finish it and
verify it at phone width before you call it done.

Lesson: the whole method end to end. With Amadej, deploy it via Dokploy so it ends as a live URL, because that feeling is the point.

6 · The final assignment: NativeAI landing page graded by Amadej

This is the real one: your Claude researches on the web, analyzes a reference site with Playwright, extracts brand and content from our deck, and ships a landing page. Paste the prompt exactly as written. It exercises everything: the three doors, context extraction, prototyping, verification.

Rules of the road

Red line: the Nevron tool stack (Attio, Instantly, HeyReach, Apollo, dokploy-nevron, Firecrawl subscription) is never used for NativeAI work. Separate companies, separate tools, no exceptions. If a workflow seems to need one of them, the workflow gets redesigned.
RuleWhat it means in practice
Draft-first outboundAnything leaving the company (email, post, message to a client) is a draft until a human approves it. The AI writes, you send.
Least privilegeConnections get added when the work demands them, not upfront. The AI gets the intern's keys.
Everything reversibleWork lives in git. Every change is tracked, every mistake is an undo, which is exactly why we can let the AI work autonomously.
Honest status"Partially done, X remains" beats "done!". This applies to the AI (it's in your CLAUDE.md) and to us.
Tasks in NativeAI OSWork that isn't a task doesn't exist. Start it as a task, close it with a note.
